Visiting Professor

Jamie C. Macbeth

jamie.macbeth@gmail.com Room 32-258

Jamie Macbeth is Assistant Professor at Smith College, Department of Computer Science. In 2019-2020 academic year, he is a MIT Martin Luther King, Jr. Visiting Professor at Genesis Group. His research is focused on building and studying intelligent computing systems that demonstrate a human-like capability for in-depth understanding and production of natural language, and thus can achieve richer interactions with human users. He is especially keen on building systems that decompose the meaning of language into complex conceptual structures that reflect humans' embodied cognition, memory, imagery, and knowledge about social situations. He uses crowdsourcing and machine learning techniques to build these systems to scale, but he also often crafts systems by hand because it can tell us how to use crowds and machine learning methods better.

Zhutian Yang

ztyang@mit.edu Room 32-259

Yang is a first-year PhD student at the Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science at MIT. She is interested in modeling how humans learn from stories -- their life experience and the stories of others. She developed Genesis Novice Learners that acquires procedural knowledge by reading online recipes, asking questions to experts, and reading stories of problem-solving [1]. Yang is currently developing a socially intelligent system that understands players' behavior in Minecraft games as stories, then it will predicts player actions, infers players mental states, and advises players based on past experience to help them better achieve goals.

Founder and Inspirer

Patrick H. Winston

Patrick Winston (1943 - 2019) led CSAIL’s Genesis Group, which focused on developing AI systems that have human-like intelligence, including the ability to tell, perceive, and comprehend stories. He believed that such work could help illuminate aspects of human intelligence that scientists don’t yet understand. He was professor at MIT for almost 50 years, Winston was director of MIT’s Artificial Intelligence Laboratory from 1972 to 1997 before it merged with the Laboratory for Computer Science to become MIT’s Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL).
